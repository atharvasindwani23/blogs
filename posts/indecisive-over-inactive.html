Indecisive > Inactive

For the longest time we've critiqued being confused. And I've hated it.

There's a common refrain I keep hearing — from alumni, from peers, even from professors: I'm trying to do it all, and I should go all in, pour every ounce of effort into one thing.

And practically, that makes sense. Your chance of success is directly proportional to the effort you put in. More effort, more success.

The problem is that we've quietly started using two very different words as if they mean the same thing: indecisiveness and inaction.

Being confused is not the same as being idle. For the longest time, we've vilified the wrong one.

So please — celebrate being confused. It means you're thinking beyond one career, one opportunity, one "correct" decision. It means you refuse to shrink your whole life down to a single lane just because a single lane is easier to optimize.

Stop trying to hyperoptimize every single step toward a "goal" you're not even sure you want.

Celebrate being confused. Celebrate being indecisive about which path to take, which friend group to be in. Celebrate admitting that you might be wrong, and that you don't yet know how to be right.

Celebrate being you.

It's okay to make a wrong decision. In fact, it's 100% better than making no decision at all. Because the worst thing you can do when you're confused — is nothing.
